Goodbye Symbian, 30 May 2007

My ultimate posting on Symbian's Notes 'General' discussion forum

The short version, is that employee #131 is leaving Symbian after almost 9 years. Joining day 26 Oct 1998, official last day 1st of June 2007. I'll be at the Crown on Friday the 1st.

I had some amazingly good, bad, sad, geeky, innovating, bureaucratic, rewarding, mad, angry and fun days here at Symbian. It has been an amazing roller-coaster indeed, for which I'd like to thank you all. I've been through the better days of Symbian, the much worse and these days aren't either. So I'm hopping that I'm leaving you in what is going to be a ''hockey stick curve'' in terms of greatness.

Some have characterised me as passionate, arrogant, maverick, f^&&^&g a$%%e, a good friend and much much more. Above all I am an engineer who cares and have always considered Symbian family...of which members you can't always chose of course. Why do I care so much? Well it is probably my DNA, education, family, upbringing and experiences to which Psion, Psion Software and Symbian have contributed, more that it may be obvious to some :-)

I was about 8 or 9 years old and traveling to London with my parents for my annual eye tests (yes it was that bad) when I was introduced to that gray gadget with the yellow buttons, at Selfridges electronics section. Nothing else mattered after that, I had to have it; but neither my parents nor I could convince them to spend all that money.

Fast forward a dozen year or so, I was at the uni, doing my MSc and being really frustrated with the course and all that Java programming we had to do suddenly. I hadn't spent 4 years doing a BEng in Comms to be subjected to applet writing - what I needed was the job featuring in a small ad by Psion Software for mobile embedded stuff; but...I couldn't quit the course, that wasn't my style. Then on the BBC I saw how Psion had problems with a software bug in their spreadsheet app and how that caused so much pain to so many people that trusted those (black at that point) devices with their life. I still wanted that device... my Pilot Pro wasn't that good I knew it, I had to do something, but what?

It was time to enjoy my summer holidays following the extremely pressurised 9-month MSc I had just completed at Essex E.S.E. I was in the train station lounge in Athens waiting for the train to go to the north of Greece. This was when I read in the bottom left column on the tech news section of that Greek newspaper that a company named Symbian had just been formed by Ericsson, Nokia and Psion. "That was really cool!" I said and had to call my fellow geeks to tell them about it... but I had no mobile phone and wasn't close to any Internet kiosk either. I parked that excitement and went on the journey playing with my Pilot Pro and thinking of new apps that would use its brand new modem I had just purchased (hence traveling by train north and not by airplane:-). On the background I was trying to figure out what those people at Symbian were going to do and how could they fight it with 3Com(Palm) and M$. That was a great challenge, a battle I was up for.

Couple of months after that and while waiting for my girlfriend to finish from her interview, I called my agent from a booth in Slough to find out how my previous interview had gone. He said that SGI wanted me but that there was a company that could be interesting for me to see. He warned me that I may have never herd of them before, 'Symbian', was their name. To my agent's amazement I knew a lot about that company and booked me to have a telephone interview with them.

Back in Colchester waiting for that phone call felt like an eternity. Well I'had been waiting for it almost 15 years, it was an eternity! In what was the second most stressful call of my life I managed to convince that guy to see me for an interview in person this time, so he told me to come next Thursday at his office and to make sure I come dressed down, no suits or ties and stuff like that! ''Of course I said, I'm an engineer''.

Harcourt St, didn't have the most glamorous buildings and certainly Sentinel House wasn't one. But I was going to see one of the company's directors and that's all that mattered. To be honest I wasn't expecting a techie interview from a director. Little I knew David Wood had different inclinations and ideas. It wasn't a grilling excactly, but it came close, if it hadn't been for his secretary that interrupted us half way through. That gave me the chance to be nosy around his office...really. I found the red binder with the ''Epoc training'' material and I started reading it, I had to know what I was getting into you know. Well, he caught me red handed and started talking about the C++ framework. Ok I had to have this job, that was it the wait was over!

The interview continued and DW started going over all technologies I had ever touched in the uni. At some point he goes ''How much do you know about Java then?''. Java? I said to myself...oh no, ''quite'' I said and DW repeated ''quite, err ok anything else you want to talk about?''. ''Sure, are you guys doing Mobile IP at all or planning to?''. DW for the first (and only) time, blanked on me, wrapped it up and walked me to a team around his office. It was actually lunch time, so the Lavender and Roxette teams were of course at the local pub so I was introduced to the system documentation folk only; that was an anti-climax but it didn't matter, I knew I was in!

Going home I felt something wasn't right, I couldn't tell what though. Next morning I knew what was bothering me, I wanted to write code. why did he introduce me to the docs folk? I didn't want to do that!! So In what was the most stressful and difficult phone call of my like I called DW. I told him I wasn't going to take the job. Wot!!!??? Yup, I did. In those eternal milliseconds passed before DW spoke, I felt the black hole underneath me pulling me fast. Then, _patiently_ he reassured me that I was going to write code for a very interesting team in 'L.P.D'. ''ahh ok then, I accept the offer:-)''.

On Oct 26 '98 David Amos welcomed me into Symbian, in what was an amazing LPD team. I got the best possible training a new guy could have, from two of the company's best engineers and mentors, David and Joe Branton were responsible for my development, because Boss Hogg had figured out immediately I was ''one of them'' :-). They gave me a Lavender proto, a company SIM, a Psion S5 and then in the memorable words of David Amos, showed me the way... ''John, this is the debugger. the debugger is your friend, I'll be over there''.

The rest is history as they say...

At Symbian I had some amazing time that had to do exclusively with my team-mates and bosses, such as the Lavender and Roxette team, the original Bluetooth team, the ADP/Comms/LBS team and the SDN team. Those teams were fun, innovating and productive because ''managers'' like David Wood, Duncan Hogg, David Amos, Mal Minhas, Mark Heath, Ian Weston, Colin Turfus and Amanda Soutter enabled us and did not ''manage'' us (or that was my perception at least:-). People in these teams have been friends, coaches and mentors. They were harsh but fair and they were keen to learn and stand corrected. They also have courage and ethos. My ugly times at Symbian were directly correlated to the opposite style of management, so I'll not mention the names, to protect the guilty (but there is always Panos's blog to read:-).

It has been a long journey for a kid in Athens to come and create in London the gadgets he could never have in the 80s. A very long way indeed. This, I hope now gives you a belated explanation of why the passion and might every time I did anything, had an opinion or criticised Symbian. Therefore I have no regrets, I had a lot of fun.

In the words of Jonathan Dixon ''I can do more good to Symbian and Symbian OS now from the outside, than I can do from the inside. So I'm off.''

thanx :-)

Indicative of Symbian's rot and demise at the time of my departure was the fact that some executive with higher permission on the Lotus Notes system than the rest of us, deleted my goodbye posting from the newgroups.

This had happenned before with more opinionated postings by engineers that cryed out loud for Symbian to shape up and look beyond its bureaucratic ways and Nokia's arrogance...

By 2007 Psion's crown jewels had given way to bad code that had littered the whole of the stack, a very poor Nokia-led S60 GUI and some lame management worth of every Dilbert mockery.
